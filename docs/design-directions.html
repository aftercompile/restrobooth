RestroBooth · Phase 0 · Design gate

Three directions. Pick one.

A restaurant OS has to be three products at once: a cinematic thing a guest meets for ninety seconds, a dense thing a cashier stares at for ten hours, and a calm thing an owner reads on a Sunday. One design system, three densities. These are the three ways I'd do it.

How to judge these. Don't ask which screenshot you like. Ask which signature element still earns its place on the KDS — the screen a chef reads across a hot line, where every pixel is a ticket you can or cannot see. That question decides it, and it decides it against my own instincts about which of these is prettiest.

The constraints, as tests

Pass or fail — not aspirations

ConstraintThe test it has to pass
One system, three densities The same tokens produce Booth, POS + KDS, and Console. If a direction needs different tokens per surface, it has failed.
Boldness in one place Name the signature element in one sentence. If you can't, there isn't one.
Animation-free POS A 200 ms transition on a billing screen is a bug. The cashier hits a key 400 times a shift and all 400 must land instantly. Speed is the aesthetic there. A direction that needs motion to feel finished has failed.
Not a template No cream + serif + terracotta. No near-black + acid green. No glassmorphism, no gradient hero, no broadsheet hairlines. These are the current AI-design defaults and they read as a tell.
Quality floor WCAG AA. Visible focus. 44 px touch targets on POS. prefers-reduced-motion. Tabular numerals wherever digits stack.

Ticket Rail

Archivo · Inter · IBM Plex Mono

The restaurant's native document isn't a dashboard card — it's a ticket. So the ticket becomes the layout primitive: a fixed measure, a perforated edge, a monospace data spine. The app ground is the dark board the tickets hang on. Inverting the obvious (dark board, white ticket, rather than cream receipt paper everywhere) is what keeps it out of diner pastiche — and what sidesteps the banned cream palette by construction.

Signature

The state rail. Every entity — a table, a ticket, a bill row, an outlet in a report — carries a 4 px rail on its leading edge, and the rail's colour is its state. Nothing else in the interface is allowed to encode state with colour.

The rail's fill: a time-temperature ramp

Cool when fresh, hot when late. Borrowed from Direction C — it's the best single idea in this document and it deserves to survive its direction. Constrained here to drive only the rail, never the whole surface. Colour is never the only channel: the rail always sits beside a numeric age, and the critical state adds a hatch, so it survives colour-blindness and a glare-lit kitchen screen.

The menu paints instantly. This rail slides in when the AI is ready — or never, and nothing breaks.

Works
  • The signature is information, not decoration. It's the only direction whose signature makes the dense surfaces better rather than merely tolerating them.
  • Survives all three densities as one system: 4 px on KDS, 6 px on a console row, a glowing card edge on the Booth.
  • Leaves the colour budget free. Because only the rail encodes state, everything else can be quiet. That is what "spend the boldness in one place" actually means.
  • Refuses every banned default without trying to — it's derived from the industry's material culture, not from a palette database.
Breaks
  • It's quiet. It will not win a design award and it does not make a great screenshot. I think that's the right trade for software someone stares at for ten hours — but it is a real cost.
  • Enamel + brass could tip "heritage/colonial" if the photography and copy go wrong. Mitigation: contemporary food photography, sentence case, zero ornament, no serif anywhere.
  • Brass fails AA on light (2.2:1). So brass is a fill, never light-mode text — enamel-700 (7.6:1) carries text there. A real constraint, encoded as a lint rule, not discovered in a Phase 10 audit.

The isometry has to be abandoned here — so the signature does not, in fact, survive all three densities.

Works
  • The most functionally motivated idea of the three. It encodes the thing the product actually cares about — elapsed time, attention — directly into colour, across four features already in the roadmap.
  • Spectacular on the Floor surface, and a genuinely great demo.
Breaks
  • It spends the entire colour budget on one semantic axis. Once cool-to-warm means time, colour can't safely mean anything else — including veg/non-veg, which in India is a legally-coded green/brown mark and is not negotiable. A system that has already spent green on "time" fights the law of the land on every menu screen.
  • Isometric is a trap at POS density. It is beautiful and inefficient with screen area — the exact wrong trade on a tablet at 9 PM. It degrades to a plan view on POS, which means the signature fails the core constraint.
  • Dark-first in a brightly-lit dining room is a glare problem. Kitchens are bright. This wants dimming, and dimming isn't available.
Recommendation

Direction B — Service Board

It is the only direction whose signature element makes the dense surfaces better instead of merely surviving them — and the dense surfaces are the product.

The dense surfaces decide it

A cashier looks at the POS for ten hours; a chef reads the KDS across a hot line. A's perforation costs pixels there. C's isometry costs area there and has to be abandoned. B's rail costs 4 px and tells you something true. That asymmetry is the whole argument.

The signature is information

"Spend the boldness in one place" is usually read as put one beautiful thing on the page. The stronger reading is put one meaningful thing everywhere. A cashier, a chef and an owner learn the rail once and read it on every surface.

The colour budget stays free

Because only the rail encodes state, the rest of the UI can be quiet. C cannot say that — and India's legally-coded veg/non-veg marks mean the colour budget is not actually ours to spend twice.

What I'm taking from the losers

From C, the time-temperature ramp, as the rail's fill — the best single idea here, and it deserves to survive its direction. From A, the split-flap order-status board on the Booth: motion, in the one place motion belongs.

Where I could be wrong — overrule me here

Direction B will not win a design award and it will not make a good screenshot. It is quiet. If the goal is a product that makes a restaurateur gasp in a demo, C is the better choice and I would build it without complaint.

I'm recommending B because I think the person who has to look at it for ten hours matters more than the person who looks at it for ten seconds. But that is a judgment about who we're building for, and it's yours to make, not mine.

Two honest notes on this page. The brief asks me to read a frontend-design skill; it doesn't exist in this environment, so I used ui-ux-pro-max for the quality floor (contrast, touch targets, tabular numerals, reduced motion, no colour-only meaning) and designed the directions myself — a generic palette database is precisely where the banned AI-design tells come from. Separately: this page's sandbox blocks external font CDNs, so the specimens render in system faces. The named typefaces are the specification, not what you're looking at. Colour, the rail, the ramp, and the density behaviour — the things that actually decide this gate — are exactly as they would ship.
